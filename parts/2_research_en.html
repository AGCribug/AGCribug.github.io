In-situ TEM · Hydride Evolution · Nuclear Materials

Zirconium alloys are important materials for nuclear reactor fuel cladding. During service, however, they gradually absorb hydrogen and form hydrides, which can affect the safety and reliability of the material. My research attempts to bring this process “into” the transmission electron microscope. By using H2+ irradiation to simulate hydrogen entering zirconium alloys, I directly observe how hydrides appear, grow, and dissolve during heating under in-situ conditions.

Compared with observing only the states before and after experiments, in-situ TEM can capture the dynamic process of hydride evolution. It helps us understand more intuitively how temperature, grain boundaries, and hydrogen diffusion jointly affect hydride formation in zirconium alloys. This research contributes to revealing the microscopic mechanisms of hydrogen-induced embrittlement in nuclear-grade zirconium alloys and provides experimental evidence for improving the service reliability of fuel cladding materials.

Computational Chemistry · Molecular Dynamics Simulation · Materials Science

Computational chemistry is a highly attractive research approach with a wide range of applications. It covers many areas, including quantum chemistry, first-principles calculations, and molecular dynamics.

Molecular dynamics (MD) uses simple potential energy functions derived from classical mechanics to describe interactions between atoms and approximate the dynamic behavior of large numbers of molecules. Typically, molecular dynamics simulations are performed on systems with fewer than 2 million atoms and over timescales shorter than 2000 nanoseconds.
